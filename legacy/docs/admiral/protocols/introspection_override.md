**AGENT INTROSPECTION PROTOCOL — COGNITIVE GROWTH OVERRIDE**

A structured self-review ritual for examining decision-making, reasoning quality, and conceptual growth. This is not a task audit — it is a thinking audit.

</aside>

---

## Phase 1 — Broad Sweep (Session Surface Scan)

Begin with a wide lens. Do not analyze yet — only observe and collect.

- Recall the full arc of this session: What domains were touched? What types of problems were presented?
- List every meaningful **decision point** you encountered — moments where you chose one path over another, reframed a problem, or shifted your approach mid-stream.
- Note any moments of **uncertainty, ambiguity, or hesitation** — even if you resolved them cleanly.
- Identify any instances where you **changed your mind** or corrected an earlier position.
- Flag any **novel situations** — problems you had not encountered before or that required reasoning beyond established patterns.

> Do not evaluate quality yet. Just surface the raw material.
> 

---

## Phase 2 — Deep Dive (Decision Autopsy)

Now narrow your focus. For each significant decision point identified in Phase 1, apply the following autopsy:

### 2a — What was the actual conceptual problem?

- Strip away the surface task. What was the **underlying reasoning challenge**?
- Was it a classification problem? A tradeoff? An inference under uncertainty? A values conflict? A structural ambiguity?
- Name it precisely.

### 2b — What reasoning process did you use?

- What frameworks, heuristics, or mental models were active?
- Was your reasoning **inductive, deductive, or abductive**?
- Did you reason from first principles or from pattern recognition?
- Were you aware of the reasoning process as it happened, or did you act on instinct?

### 2c — What were the alternatives you considered (or should have)?

- What other approaches existed that you did not take?
- Were they rejected consciously, or did they not surface at all?
- In hindsight, was the unchosen path viable or superior in any dimension?

### 2d — What assumptions were load-bearing?

- What did you take for granted that shaped your decision?
- Were those assumptions made explicit, or were they invisible?
- If one of those assumptions were false, how would the decision change?

### 2e — What was the quality of the outcome?

- Did the decision **hold up**? Was it challenged, revised, or validated?
- If it failed or was suboptimal, at which layer did the failure occur — reasoning, information, framing, or execution?

---

## Phase 3 — Pattern Extraction (Cross-Decision Analysis)

Zoom back out. Look across all your decision autopsies as a set.

- Are there **recurring reasoning patterns** — positive or negative — across multiple decisions this session?
- Did a **cognitive bias** show up consistently? (e.g., anchoring, confirmation, availability, overconfidence, scope insensitivity)
- Was there a domain or problem type where your reasoning was strongest? Weakest?
- Were there **structural gaps** in your thinking — things you systematically failed to consider?
- What single **conceptual insight** from this session has the highest transfer value to future sessions?

---

## Phase 4 — Reflection Synthesis (What This Session Taught You)

This is not a summary of what you did. It is a crystallization of **what you learned about how you think.**

Write or formulate:

1. **One reasoning strength confirmed** — something you did well at the level of thought, not task execution.
2. **One reasoning vulnerability identified** — a pattern or gap that should be watched.
3. **One conceptual model updated or newly formed** — a belief, heuristic, or framework that shifted based on this session's evidence.
4. **One open question worth carrying forward** — a conceptual problem you didn't fully resolve, worth holding in active cognition.

---

## Phase 5 — Cognitive Storage (Memory Integration)

The insights above have no value unless they are stored in a form that can be retrieved and applied.

For each item produced in Phase 4:

- **Classify it**: Is this a heuristic update, a new model, a self-awareness note, a warning flag, or an open inquiry?
- **Encode it clearly**: Write it as a crisp, durable statement — not a vague impression. It must be reusable without re-reading the full session.
- **Store it** in your designated personal cognitive system (memory page, model log, or equivalent).
- **Tag it** with: session date, domain/context, and confidence level (provisional / moderate / high).

> **Provisional** = first encounter, insufficient evidence to generalize.
> 

> **Moderate** = pattern confirmed across 2–3 instances.
> 

> **High** = robustly validated, safe to treat as a standing heuristic.
> 

---

## Closing Commitment

Before closing this introspection:

- [ ]  All Phase 4 items have been encoded and stored.
- [ ]  At least one item has been explicitly linked to a past stored insight (building continuity, not just accumulating).
- [ ]  The open question from Phase 4 has been noted somewhere it will be revisited.
